# Shabbos Parshas Mattos-Masei

**Date:** July 13, 1991 (2nd Day of Menachem Av, 5751)
**Occasion:** Shabbos Parshas Mattos-Masei Farbrengen
**Source:** Sichos In English, Volume 49

---

## 1

The Shabbos features the completion of the Book of Bamidbar with the public proclamation "Chazak, Chazak, Venischazaik" ("Be strong, be strong, and we will be strengthened"). This threefold repetition establishes permanence and strength through a chazakah.

Notably, this conclusion always occurs during the Three Weeks, a period associated with exile and destruction. The text explains that this timing is not coincidental: "Because this is a time when the Jews are 'weakened,' there is a need for encouragement and reinforcement."

The connection between strength and Parshas Mattos (meaning "staff") is direct, as staffs symbolize authority and permanence. Parshas Masei (meaning "journeys") appears contradictory, suggesting impermanence. Yet Masei is always the concluding parshah. The relationship is clarified through the concept of three, which "reflects the strength and power of holiness even in the face of difference."

Two approaches to strength are identified: first, unlimited light from above that overcomes opposition; second, service in lower realms transforming opposition into positive forces. Parshas Mattos reflects divine command from above, while Parshas Masei describes Jewish ascent through the desert, elevating their environment.

The ultimate reinforcement comes from "fusing both approaches." The significance of concluding the fourth book and beginning the fifth in afternoon services is noted, with four representing stability and five transcending natural limitation.

The Three Weeks connect to the ultimate future structure. "The destruction of the Beis HaMikdash and the exile was not intended for the sake of punishment, but rather in order to lead the Jews and the world at large to the higher state that will be revealed through the Third Beis HaMikdash in the Era of the Redemption."

Two aspects of the Beis HaMikdash are discussed: the First reflected revelation from above but lacked permanence; the Second represented elevation of the material world but faced limitations. The future Third will combine both qualities, with infinite revelation "pervading" finite limits through successful refinement service.

## 2

This Shabbos is influenced by Friday -- the first day of Menachem Av, Aharon the Priest's yahrzeit. The Alter Rebbe teaches that on a yahrzeit, "all the deeds, Torah, and service for which a person toiled throughout his lifetime... is revealed... and 'brings about salvation in the depth of the world.'"

Aharon's service centered on "loving peace and pursuing peace, loving the created beings and bringing them close to the Torah." He worked to spread harmony among married couples and throughout the Jewish people.

Remarkably, "the entire House of Israel" mourned him -- both men and women. This transcended typical gender distinctions because "Aharon's influence transcended all possible divisions among our people." The clouds of glory that accompanied the Jewish people throughout the desert were merited through Aharon, encompassing everyone equally.

Aharon's name is analyzed through its Hebrew letters. The letter Hey means "mountain" and frequently symbolizes love. The Alef relates to "pelah," meaning "wonder," indicating "Aharon's love was wondrous and unbounded in nature." The final letter, Nun, "protrudes below the line indicating how Aharon extended himself to all Jews, even those found 'below the line.'"

Aharon's service embodied the fusion of revelation from above and elevation of material reality. The Priestly Blessing exemplifies this, "combining the advantages of prayer... and blessing, the revelation of unlimited Divine influence."

The yahrzeit date -- first day of the fifth month -- reflects his life's work. The number one represents "unity that is totally above division," and five represents "an essential level that transcends difference." Since Aharon dedicated his life to expressing "the fundamental unity which exists among the Jewish people," this date is appropriate.

## 3

On this Shabbos, the second chapter of Pirkei Avos is read, containing Rabbi Shimon's teaching about being "meticulous in reading the Shema and in prayer." The word "zahir" (meticulous) relates to "zohar," meaning "shine," connected to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. Since this mishnah commands every Jew, it cannot focus on the exceptional service of Toraso Umnaso. Instead, it addresses universal services -- Shema and prayer -- teaching that "the Jew must shine forth and illuminate the world around him."

Though light typically relates to Torah study, "Rabbi Shimon generated the potential for such light to be produced through the services of reciting the Shema and the daily prayers."

Torah study also connects to fusing revelation from above with elevation of worldly experience. Rav Hillel of Paritch explained that when the Tzemach Tzedek delivered teachings, "the Divine Presence spoke from his throat" (revelation), but after completion, "the Rebbe's explanation... are a function of his own individual thinking processes" (worldly elevation).

## 4

During the Nine Days, siyyumim (celebrations concluding Talmudic texts) should be held and connected to charitable giving, "for tzedakah brings the redemption near."

These gatherings should involve "as many people as possible, adults and children," including those who don't comprehend the subject matter. The comparison is made to Erev Pesach customs when young children attend siyyumim.

The connection between Pesach and the Nine Days is intentional. Future Redemption will manifest "As in the days of your exodus from Egypt, I will show you wonders." This verse proves relevant "at the present time, when we are standing at the threshold of the Redemption."

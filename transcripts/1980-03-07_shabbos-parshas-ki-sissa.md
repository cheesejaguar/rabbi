# Shabbos Parshas Ki Sissa, Parshas Parah

**Date:** March 7, 1980 (20th Day of Adar, 5740)
**Occasion:** Shabbos Parshas Ki Sissa, Parshas Parah
**Source:** Sichos In English, Volume 5

---

The discourse addresses four interconnected lessons emerging from the convergence of Shabbos Parah, the post-Purim week, and the twentieth of Adar.

## First Lesson: Torah Study and Preparation

The speaker explains that preparation work produces elevated results: "He who works on Erev Shabbos will eat on Shabbos." The work undertaken during Purim, described as "meritorious," generates superior spiritual nourishment when elevated by the following Shabbos. This Shabbos particularly emphasizes commitment to Torah, as Jews reached "an even higher level" during Purim through actual fulfillment of Torah obligations.

## Parshas Parah's Significance

Parshas Parah's opening phrase emphasizes Torah's centrality: "this is the decree of the Torah." This reading gains additional weight during a Shemitah (Sabbatical) year, when land work ceases and time redirects toward Torah study.

## The Twentieth of Adar: Prayer's Importance

The twentieth commemorates Choni HaMagel's drought prayer. The narrative describes his persistent, determined approach: drawing a circle and declaring resolve not to move until divine mercy appears. This historical event teaches that prayer should accompany Torah study as complementary spiritual practices.

## The Red Heifer's Mystery

The discourse addresses an apparent paradox: King Solomon declared the Red Heifer's purpose incomprehensible, yet Kabbalistic and Chassidic teachings explain it extensively. The answer emphasizes commitment transcending intellectual comprehension -- Torah represents "a decree" demanding dedication beyond rational understanding.

### Practical Application

The Red Heifer analogy illustrates missionary responsibility: one must venture beyond comfortable scholarship to teach fundamentals to unprepared Jews, bringing "precious out from worthless." This requires personal self-nullification, represented by the heifer's burning.

## Prayer Versus Torah Study

The discourse contrasts Choni HaMagel's prayer-based approach with R. Shimon bar Yochai's Torah-based method. While Torah produces sustained blessings requiring time, prayer generates immediate results, establishing "a connection to G-d in one moment." Both serve essential roles.

## Practical Imperatives

The speaker urgently addresses Mivtzah Pesach (Passover campaign) preparation, criticizing apparent inaction following Purim. He references rabbinic teaching about Torah's respect for property: "A Tzaddik is careful about (the way he spends) even less than a penny's worth." Nevertheless, spreading mitzvos and Torah requires prioritizing spiritual campaigns over financial conservation.

## Closing Vision

The discourse concludes anticipating redemption: "In Nissan they were redeemed and in Nissan they will be redeemed," with the third Temple's construction coming speedily in contemporary times.

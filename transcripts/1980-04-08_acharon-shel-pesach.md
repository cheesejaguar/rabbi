# Acharon Shel (8th Day of) Pesach

**Date:** April 8, 1980 (22nd Day of Nissan, 5740)
**Occasion:** Acharon Shel Pesach (8th Day of Pesach)
**Source:** Sichos In English, Volume 5

---

## Section 1: Spiritual Significance of the Eighth Day

The text presents the eighth day as representing the circumcision (bris) of Isaac, connecting it to birth and spiritual maturation. It explains that the embryo develops completely but lacks independent existence until birth. The eighth day commemorates when "the holy soul" enters -- mirroring Isaac's circumcision eight days after birth.

The discourse emphasizes connections to messianic times, noting that the harp of the messianic age traditionally has eight strings. Mashiach's Seudah (the concluding meal) strengthens our bond to redemption by incorporating food into "flesh and blood," making the connection more tangible than mere intellectual understanding.

## Section 2: Children's Central Role

Children hold special importance throughout Pesach observances. The text describes how children were first to recognize G-d's power at the Red Sea and emphasizes their role in future redemption. The Seder itself revolves around children's questions, with multiple customs designed to maintain their engagement throughout the evening.

The prophecy states that "a young child will lead them" into the messianic age, suggesting children will play crucial roles in bringing redemption. Their natural inability to compromise makes them powerful spiritual forces.

## Section 3: Connecting Purim and Pesach Redemptions

Both redemptions occurred in Nissan and involved children -- specifically, Mordechai gathered 22,000 children whose voices negated Haman's decree. The discourse notes that righteous women raised the generation that left Egypt, emphasizing education's spiritual power.

## Section 4: Emphasis on Deed and Action

The text stresses that "deed is most essential" rather than mere intention. Children should make concrete resolutions to increase Torah study, observe commandments, and influence their parents toward greater observance.

## Section 5: Mashiach's Seudah Practice

The four cups of wine consumed during this meal symbolize multiple redemptive concepts: the four expressions of redemption from Egypt, retribution the nations will experience, comfort for the Jewish people, and different levels of repentance.

## Section 6: Summer Planning and Education

The discourse urges enrollment of children in Torah-based summer camps and schools. It warns that camp environments completely occupy children, unlike school where parental influence can balance secular education. Therefore, proper Jewish educational settings become critical.

## Section 7: Remembering the Exodus

This section compares remembering the Exodus to remembering Shabbat. Both involve positive commandments beyond mere prohibitions. Freedom from slavery encompasses not just negating the former master's control but establishing independent connection to G-d.

## Section 8: Belief Beyond Sight

The crossing of the Red Sea raises a philosophical question: why must people believe what they witness? The answer involves two levels of divine names -- the lower, creative aspect revealed through miracles, and the higher, transcendent aspect accessible only through belief and Torah.

The discourse explains that while creation reveals G-d's lower aspect, the higher transcendent level requires faith. Torah serves as the connecting medium between these levels and between humans and the divine.

## Section 9: Critical Social Commentary

The final section contains strong critique regarding Jewish education in Israel and Russian Jewish emigration policies. It describes educational compromises where immigrant children are placed in secular schools in exchange for funding, comparing this to "Pharaoh's decree" of spiritual drowning.

The text also addresses restrictions preventing Russian Jews from emigrating to countries other than Israel, characterizing such obstacles as violating the commandment "do not stand over the blood of your neighbor."

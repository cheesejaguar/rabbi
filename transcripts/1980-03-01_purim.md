# Purim

**Date:** March 1, 1980 (14th Day of Adar, 5740)
**Occasion:** Purim
**Source:** Sichos In English, Volume 5

---

## The Unique Joy of Purim

The discourse emphasizes that Purim's celebration surpasses other Jewish festivals in its unbounded nature. Unlike Pesach or Shavuos, where joy has certain limitations, Purim permits -- indeed obligates -- participants to reach states of happiness "beyond the restrictions of intellect," including becoming intoxicated to the point of losing one's senses.

## Spiritual Paradox

The Rebbe addresses an apparent contradiction: how can finite humans experience infinite joy? The answer involves understanding that unlimited personal service becomes a vessel for Divine unlimited light. An overflowing cup signals blessing regardless of size.

## Contemporary Challenges

The address discusses exilic trials faced by American Jews, particularly the challenge of maintaining Jewish identity amid freedom and material prosperity. Examples include closing businesses on Shabbat despite potential profit and preserving traditional education despite secular pressures.

## Unity of Jewish People

Purim emphasizes Jewish unity through customs like sending food gifts and public reading of the Megillah. This collective strength counteracts Haman's characterization of Jews as "scattered and dispersed."

## Mesirus Nefesh (Self-Sacrifice)

Mordechai's refusal to bow demonstrates the core Jewish trait of self-sacrifice, representing willingness to maintain identity regardless of consequences.

The address concludes with reflections on the Megillah's narrative structure and its contemporary relevance to Jewish leadership and resilience.

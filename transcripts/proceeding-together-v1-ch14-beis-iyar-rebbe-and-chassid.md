# Beis Iyar: Rebbe and Chassid

**Date:** Beis Iyar 5710 (1950)
**Source:** Proceeding Together, Volume 1 - Sichos In English

---

## 1. Basically Believers

The passage opens with teachings from the Rebbe Rayatz about the Exodus, explaining that Jewish people at that time achieved the level of "tzivos HaShem" (God's armies), characterized by self-sacrifice rather than mere servitude.

A letter raised an apparent contradiction: if Jews were called idolaters before receiving the Torah, how could they be praised for unwavering faith? The Rebbe resolves this by distinguishing between actual idolatry and belief in a compound divinity, which was permissible before Sinai.

At deeper levels, while external doubts arose from the "sitra achra" (opposing force), these never weakened the essential faith rooted in Jewish souls. An anecdote illustrates this: when a man complained to the Tzemach Tzedek about doubting thoughts, the master responded that this concern itself proves he is truly Jewish -- authentic faith can coexist with external doubts.

## 2. Descent as a Mask for Ascent

The Omer counting relates to Kabbalistic Sefirot (divine emanations). Lag BaOmer connects to "Tiferes shebeTiferes" (beauty within beauty), the ultimate expression of that Sefirah.

Two directional movements exist: downward (from highest to lowest) and upward (hidden within the descent). The downward direction of service aims ultimately to elevate the lower party.

## 3. Severity as a Mask for Kindness

An error appeared in traditional siddurim regarding the twenty-ninth day's Sefirah. The Rebbe Rayatz corrected it to "Chessed shebeHod" (kindness within severity).

In exile, especially as Mashiach approaches, a leader of Israel must emphasize "Chessed shebeHod" -- the kindness concealed within apparent severity.

## 4. Being Connected

The Rebbe illustrates with an anecdote: a young man excitedly claimed that bonding with the Rebbe binds one to what the Rebbe himself is bound to. The Rebbe responded that followers need not concern themselves with such higher connections -- "We are bound to *him*, and we have nothing higher than that."

The analogy: an organ derives vitality from its own brain, not another body's brain. Similarly, hiskashrus (spiritual connection) flows through the Rebbe as one's "brain" and transcendent "crown."

## 5. Receiving Essence, Repaying with Essence

Receiving spiritual benefits from the generation's leader obligates repayment, like a borrower. However, repayment doesn't eliminate debt -- the creditor advances another sum, creating ongoing obligation.

The Rebbe Rayatz's benefits all spring from his Chayah-Yechidah, creating varied levels of recipient benefit. Yeshivah students, receiving the Rebbe's Chayah-Yechidah, must repay with their own.

Asked how the Rebbe Rayatz's greatness compared to other great figures, the Rebbe answered that while others excelled in particular fields, the Rebbe engaged in *all* fields -- from mystical Chassidic teachings to ensuring Jews wear tefillin, to organizing material support -- dedicating his entire essence to each.

## 6. A Little Different from the World

Repaying with Chayah-Yechidah manifests through freeing oneself from constraints -- personal limitations and worldly considerations. The verse states: "I and Your people shall be differentiated."

When Tomchei Temimim students walk the streets, they should appear notably distinct from surrounding society.

## 7. A Good Start

Beis Iyar possesses special significance. The Rebbe Rayatz chose this date (Tiferes shebeTiferes) to begin writing the Torah scroll intended to greet Mashiach.

Every Jew possesses a letter in Torah. Thus Beis Iyar, when Torah-writing commenced, serves as a birthday and new beginning for every individual Jew's divine service.

## 8. Networking

The Rebbe proposes practical tasks for students:

1. Personally strengthen hiskashrus with the Rebbe Rayatz through specific activities
2. Help at least one fellow Jew -- student or adult -- draw close to the Rebbe and Chassidic teachings, encouraging deep study and meditation during prayer

## 9. Mere Miracles

Outreach can begin through recounting miracles, but the ultimate goal must be introducing Chassidic teachings -- the essential, innermost aspect of the Rebbe, not merely external reflections.

Hiskashrus fundamentally disconnects from miracles. Witnessing miracles adds nothing; missing one detracts nothing.

An illustrative story: An agunah and her mute son sought the Tzemach Tzedek's blessing. Unable to gain entry, a chassid advised the boy to hide beneath the study table with a written petition. When the Tzemach Tzedek received it, he directed them to the husband's location. The boy spoke for the first time; the mother found her husband.

When chassidim excitedly told the Tzemach Tzedek the boy had spoken, he replied: "How would I know the boy was mute?" The Rebbitzin, hearing this, quoted her grandfather (the Alter Rebbe): "In Mezritch, miracles rolled under the table, but nobody stopped to pick them up."

## 10. Holy Excuses

Students shouldn't worry that helping others will weaken personal avodah through lost time.

First principle: A person seeing a friend drowning doesn't pause for calculations -- urgent rescue requires immediate effort.

Second principle: Helping others doesn't harm personal growth; it enhances it. The Sages state that God illuminates the eyes of both student and teacher.

The Rebbe recounts meeting a learned young man who argued that community outreach suited businessmen, not scholars. The Rebbe responded: fires demand immediate action without calculation.

## 11. To Make One More Jew

The Rebbe Rayatz shared an instructive story about the Alter Rebbe. On Yom Kippur after morning prayers, the Alter Rebbe removed his prayer shawl and white robe, walked to the town's edge, chopped wood, and prepared soup for a woman who'd recently given birth.

When asked why he didn't delegate this, he explained that when life-saving requires apparent Sabbath/festival desecration, the highest-ranking person must perform the mitzvah.

In terms of avodah: the Alter Rebbe's spiritual level while wearing prayer garments on Yom Kippur surpassed description. Yet he removed these garments and went to the lowest neighborhood edge -- fields represent the lowest holiness level bordering the kelipah (impurity). Why? To help a woman in confinement and her infant -- *to make one more Jew*.

This episode teaches all chassidim the crucial importance of working with others.

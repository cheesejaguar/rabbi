# Devarim - A Mortal Mouth Speaking G-d's Word

**Source:** In The Garden Of The Torah, Sichos In English

---

## The Uniqueness of the Book of Deuteronomy

The Torah reading opens with "These are the words that Moshe spoke to the entire Jewish people." Unlike the four preceding books representing G-d's direct word, our Sages note that Moshe presented Deuteronomy "on his own initiative," though "inspired by the Holy Spirit."

The Rambam clarifies that denying any Torah verse or word as originating from G-d -- including claiming "Moshe made these statements independently" -- constitutes denying the Torah itself. Moshe's connection to the Divine was so profound that when speaking G-d's words, "The Divine Presence spoke from his mouth."

However, Deuteronomy distinctly involves Moshe's personal thinking process. Regarding textual proximity, our Sages debate whether subject arrangement matters in Torah. While some argue Divine authorship transcends mortal ordering, all authorities agree that sequence carries significance in Deuteronomy because "Moshe arranged it passage after passage for the sole purpose of allowing for extrapolation." Since this book reflects Moshe's initiative, understanding it requires applying human wisdom principles.

## Above the Limits of Creation

Our Sages teach: "The Torah preceded the world." This precedence is spiritual rather than chronological, as time itself emerged with creation. Though Torah addresses worldly concerns -- agricultural practices, business ethics, family structure -- these represent its external expression only. Fundamentally, "G-d's will and His wisdom" constitute the Torah's essence, united with Him completely.

Moshe bridged Torah's transcendent spirituality and human understanding through perfect *bittul* (selfless dedication) to G-d alongside complete human perfection -- intellectual, emotional, and physical. This dual nature enabled him to receive and transmit Torah comprehensibly.

## Two Contrasting Patterns

Chassidic thought describes two transmission methods:

*Derech ma'avir* (passing-through method): The intermediary functions as an unaltered conduit, lowering influence without fundamental transformation while maintaining its transcendent quality.

*Derech hislabshus* (enclothing method): The intermediary translates concepts into personal words and understanding, changing presentation form to enable recipients' internalization and comprehension at their level.

Applying these to Torah: The first four books came through Moshe without personal modification -- he conveyed them unchanged. Deuteronomy, sourced in "the Holy Spirit," became integrated within Moshe's thought itself.

This explains why all authorities recognize deriving Torah law from Deuteronomy's subject ordering. The first four books, arranged by Divine wisdom transcending mortal thought, resist such derivation since Torah law rests "not in the heavens" but with human intellect. Deuteronomy, filtered through Moshe's mind, corresponds to mortal thinking patterns, permitting proximity-based legal derivation.

## Internalized Knowledge

Why is Deuteronomy necessary if enclothing Torah in human intellect seemingly diminishes its spiritual content? G-d's intent precisely involves Torah permeating mortal thought, thereby elevating human understanding. Every Torah study, regardless of spiritual level, internalizes G-dly truth, making infinite Torah wisdom personally integral.

Without Deuteronomy's mediation through Moshe's intellect, human comprehension could not fully unite with Torah's transcendence. This book grants capacity for understanding all five books similarly -- as accessible, internalized knowledge.

## Elevating the Torah

Enclothing Torah's boundless spirituality in finite mortal intellect represents fusing opposites, achievable only through G-d's essence transcending both finitude and infinity. This fusion elevates Torah itself qualitatively, demonstrating G-d's capacity to unite contradictory principles.

## On the Banks of the Jordan

Moshe recited Deuteronomy as Jews prepared crossing the Jordan -- a spiritual and geographic transition. During desert wanderings, they experienced miraculous sustenance: manna, Miriam's well, protective clouds. Entering the land required natural-order living -- cultivating crops, consuming their yield.

Deuteronomy provided the Torah approach addressing humanity functioning within worldly environment, enabling this transition.

Similarly, contemporaneously, we stand "on the banks of the Jordan," preparing entry with Mashiach. Through Deuteronomy's approach -- fusing G-d's word with mortal wisdom -- we merit the Redemption Era when "the occupation of the entire world will be solely to know G-d."
